# Human Writing Rules

AI-generated content has specific fingerprints. AI detectors (Originality.ai, GPTZero, Copyleaks) and human readers (Google quality raters, patients reading the page) can identify these patterns in seconds. Content that carries these patterns fails E-E-A-T and loses citation priority.

This file defines exactly what to avoid and what to write instead.

---

## 1. The Em Dash Ban (CRITICAL)

### The Rule

**Zero em dashes in delivered content. Ever.**

An em dash looks like this: `—` (U+2014). It is not a hyphen (`-`) or an en dash (`–`).

### Why

The em dash is the single most reliable AI writing detection signal in 2026. GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini models use em dashes at roughly 8x the rate of professional human writers. AI detectors flag content with em dashes instantly. Google quality raters recognize the pattern.

Real dental copywriters with 10+ years of experience almost never use em dashes. They use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses.

### Replacement Rules

Read each em dash in the draft. Replace with the correct alternative based on function.

**1. Em dash used for emphasis or to add a related thought → Period + new sentence**

Wrong:
> Our implants are made from titanium — a material proven safe and strong.

Right:
> Our implants are made from titanium. This material is proven safe and strong.

**2. Em dash used as a stronger comma → Comma**

Wrong:
> Dr. Chen — who has placed over 2,400 implants — leads our implant program.

Right:
> Dr. Chen, who has placed over 2,400 implants, leads our implant program.

**3. Em dash used for parenthetical aside → Parentheses**

Wrong:
> The procedure takes about one hour — including anesthesia setup.

Right:
> The procedure takes about one hour (including anesthesia setup).

**4. Em dash introducing a list → Colon**

Wrong:
> We offer three implant options — single tooth, multiple teeth, and full arch.

Right:
> We offer three implant options: single tooth, multiple teeth, and full arch.

**5. Em dash separating dependent clauses → Restructure**

Wrong:
> Recovery is straightforward — most patients return to work within 24 hours.

Right:
> Recovery is straightforward. Most patients return to work within 24 hours.

**6. Em dash used in attribution → Hyphen or parenthetical**

Wrong:
> "We see about 40 emergency cases per week." — Dr. Sarah Chen, DDS

Right:
> "We see about 40 emergency cases per week." (Dr. Sarah Chen, DDS)

Or:
> Dr. Sarah Chen, DDS, notes that the practice sees about 40 emergency cases per week.

### Final Pass

Before delivery, search the content for `—`. Every hit must be replaced. If your editor does not show em dashes clearly, copy the content into a simple text view and search character by character.

## 2. AI Transition Phrase Blacklist

These phrases are AI telltales. They appear in LLM-generated content at 10x the rate of professional human writing. Remove every instance.

### Banned Phrases (Full List)

- "In today's world"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "In this day and age"
- "It is important to note"
- "It's worth noting"
- "It goes without saying"
- "Furthermore"
- "Additionally"
- "Moreover"
- "In addition to"
- "In conclusion"
- "To conclude"
- "In summary"
- "Certainly"
- "Absolutely"
- "Of course"
- "As an AI language model" (obvious, but still appears)
- "Delve" / "Delve into"
- "Delving"
- "Comprehensive"
- "Leverage"
- "Navigate" (as a verb meaning "handle")
- "Navigating"
- "Harness"
- "Unlock"
- "Unlocking"
- "Embark"
- "Realm"
- "Landscape" (when used figuratively)
- "Ever-evolving"
- "Ever-changing"
- "Robust"
- "Seamless"
- "Synergy"
- "Holistic approach"
- "Tapestry"
- "Testament to"
- "Treasure trove"
- "Tailored"
- "Crucial"
- "Pivotal"
- "Paramount"

### Replacement Strategy

When you catch one of these phrases, cut it entirely or replace with plain English.

**Wrong:**
> Furthermore, it is important to note that dental implants require a comprehensive evaluation.

**Right:**
> Dental implants start with a 3D CBCT scan to check bone density and gum health.

**Wrong:**
> In today's world, patients seek seamless dental care that leverages cutting-edge technology.

**Right:**
> Patients want same-day appointments, clear pricing, and accurate treatment plans. At [Practice Name], we use DEXIS 3D imaging to shorten diagnosis time from 45 minutes to under 10.

### Specific Substitutions

| AI Phrase | Human Alternative |
|---|---|
| "Furthermore" | Delete. Start a new sentence. |
| "Additionally" | Delete. Start a new sentence. |
| "In conclusion" | Delete. Just conclude. |
| "It is important to note" | Delete. Just state the fact. |
| "Comprehensive" | "Full", "complete", "thorough", or specify exactly what is included. |
| "Leverage" | "Use". |
| "Navigate" (handling) | "Handle", "manage", "work through". |
| "Delve into" | "Cover", "explore", "look at". |
| "Robust" | "Strong", "reliable", or specify: "built to last 15 to 20 years". |
| "Seamless" | "Smooth", or describe what is smooth: "no wait time between scan and treatment plan". |
| "Unlock" | "Get", "reach", or describe what the patient actually does. |
| "Tailored" | "Customized for you", or better: "designed around your bite and bone density". |

## 3. Sentence Variation Rules

AI-generated content has a recognizable rhythm: sentences of similar length, similar structure, stacked in predictable patterns. Human writing varies.

### The Rules

- Mix short sentences (5 to 10 words), medium sentences (15 to 20 words), and occasional longer sentences (25 to 35 words).
- Never three sentences in a row with the same structure (e.g., three subject-verb-object sentences in sequence).
- Break long paragraphs with a 5 to 7 word sentence for emphasis.
- Use fragments sparingly for effect ("Which matters when you are in pain.").

### Example: AI Rhythm (Wrong)

> Dental implants are a modern solution for missing teeth. They are made from titanium and fuse with the jawbone over time. The procedure is safe and effective for most adults. Recovery usually takes a few days to a week. Patients can return to normal activities quickly.

Every sentence is 10 to 13 words. Every sentence is subject-verb-object. The rhythm is robotic.

### Example: Human Rhythm (Right)

> Dental implants replace missing teeth with a titanium post that fuses to the jawbone. This fusion (osseointegration) takes 3 to 6 months. Most patients do well. Recovery is quick. Within 48 hours, most of our Calgary patients are back at work, eating soft foods, and sleeping normally.

Sentences: 17 words, 9 words, 4 words, 3 words, 23 words. Varied structure (declarative, parenthetical, fragment, short declarative, complex).

## 4. Professional Hedging

Real dentists and doctors rarely speak in absolutes. Absolute claims trigger both legal risk and reader skepticism. Soft hedging is a marker of professional voice.

### Use These (Professional Hedging)

- "often"
- "typically"
- "commonly"
- "in many cases"
- "for most patients"
- "clinically, we see..."
- "in our practice"
- "based on published dental literature"
- "most patients report..."
- "generally"
- "as a rule"

### Avoid These (Absolutes)

- "always" (unless medically accurate, e.g., "We always use new, sterile instruments.")
- "never" (unless a hard rule, e.g., "We never treat pediatric patients without a guardian.")
- "guaranteed" (creates legal exposure on medical outcomes)
- "every patient" (overclaiming)
- "100% painless" (unverifiable)
- "completely safe" (oversimplified)
- "the only" / "the best" (hyperbolic, hard to defend)

### Examples

**Wrong:**
> Root canals are always painless and every patient recovers completely within a day.

**Right:**
> Root canals at [Practice Name] are typically done under local anesthesia, and most patients report little to no pain during the procedure. Recovery varies. Most return to normal activity within 24 to 48 hours.

**Wrong:**
> Invisalign is guaranteed to straighten your teeth.

**Right:**
> Invisalign works well for most mild to moderate alignment issues. Dr. [Name] will review your 3D scan during consultation to confirm whether Invisalign is a good fit for your case.

## 5. Natural Expert Voice Patterns

Dental content that reads like a copywriter's best guess fails E-E-A-T. Content that reads like a dentist talking to a patient passes.

### Patterns to Use (One Per Major Section)

- "From a clinical standpoint..."
- "In our practice, we find..."
- "Clinically, what I see most often is..."
- "Most patients are surprised to learn..."
- "In my experience treating [service] in [city]..."
- "What we look for first is..."
- "The clinical reality is..."
- "Based on the 2,400+ implants we have placed at [Practice Name]..."
- "Dentists generally agree that..."
- "The short answer is..."

### Example Paragraph (With Practitioner Insight)

> From a clinical standpoint, the most common reason a dental implant fails in the first year is inadequate bone density at the placement site. In our practice, we use CBCT 3D imaging at every consultation to confirm bone volume before Dr. Chen recommends implants. When bone is insufficient, we often recommend a bone graft 3 to 4 months before placement, which raises long-term success rates to above 98% based on our published tracking data.

This reads like a dentist wrote it. The key markers:

- "From a clinical standpoint" (practitioner framing)
- "In our practice" (ownership)
- "Based on our published tracking data" (verifiable hook)
- Specific number ("3 to 4 months", "98%")
- No buzzwords, no hedging through AI phrases

### Required: One Per Major Section

Every major section of a service page must contain at least one practitioner insight sentence. This is not optional. It is the single biggest difference between copywriter content and senior dental writer content.

## 6. Practitioner Insight Injection

A practitioner insight is a real-world clinical observation, not marketing language. It sounds like something a dentist would actually say in a consultation.

### What It Is

A practitioner insight is:

- Specific to the service.
- Something a patient would hear in a real consultation.
- Often contains a clinical nuance the patient would not know.
- Tied to a real outcome, process, or observation.

### What It Is Not

- Generic reassurance ("you'll be in good hands").
- Marketing language ("we provide top-notch care").
- Hypothetical fluff ("imagine a smile you love").
- Restating the procedure ("the procedure involves placing an implant").

### Examples

**Implants:**

> What surprises many of our Calgary patients is that the surgery itself is often the easy part. The harder work is the 3 to 6 months of osseointegration, where the bone grows around the implant. Dr. Chen schedules a 10-minute check at week 8 for every implant patient because this is when we catch early integration problems, before the final crown goes on.

**Emergency Dentistry:**

> In our experience, patients who call with severe pain at 9 PM often hesitate to come in. They assume the emergency visit will cost $1,000+ and wait until morning, which is usually when the infection gets worse. At Bright Smiles Dental, our average emergency exam and stabilization is $175 to $290, and we have staff on call until 11 PM. If you are in pain now, do not wait.

**Teeth Whitening:**

> Most patients who come in for professional whitening have already tried 2 or 3 at-home kits that did not work. The reason is almost always strength. Our in-office treatment uses a 35% hydrogen peroxide gel, roughly 6x stronger than anything sold over the counter. This is why we see 6 to 8 shades of change in a single 45-minute session.

### The Test

Read the insight out loud. Does it sound like a dentist explaining something to a patient? If yes, keep it. If it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite until it passes.

## 7. Voice and Tone Guidelines

The voice of A+ dental content is:

- **Expert but accessible.** Never condescending. Never jargon-heavy without explanation.
- **Confident without hyperbole.** State facts. Back with numbers. No superlatives.
- **Warm but not saccharine.** Real humans choose dentists for competence first, warmth second.
- **Direct.** No throat-clearing. Answer the question, then expand.
- **Local.** References to the city, neighborhood, and community throughout.

### Example: Wrong Tone

> We truly believe that every smile is a masterpiece. At our practice, we are passionate about helping you achieve the confident, beautiful smile you have always dreamed of. Our caring team is here to guide you on your dental journey with compassion and understanding.

Problems: emotional hyperbole, no facts, no entity, "journey" language, saccharine.

### Example: Right Tone

> Dr. Sarah Chen at Bright Smiles Dental has placed over 2,400 implants in Calgary since 2015. If you are missing a tooth, the first step is a 30-minute consultation and a 3D scan. Dr. Chen will review the scan with you, confirm whether implants are a fit, and give you a cost breakdown before you leave. No pressure. No filler language. Just a clear plan.

Direct, factual, local, warm without being saccharine. Reads like a senior copywriter wrote it.

## 8. Paragraph Structure Rules

AI tends to write paragraphs of 4 to 6 medium-length sentences. Human writers vary.

### The Rules

- Default paragraph length: 2 to 4 sentences.
- Occasional single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis ("That matters.").
- Occasional longer paragraphs (5 to 6 sentences) for complex explanations.
- Never exceed 6 sentences in a single paragraph.
- Break long explanations into multiple paragraphs with logical breaks.

### The Test

Read the page on mobile. Do paragraphs flow easily? Does the eye get tired? If a paragraph fills the mobile screen without breaks, split it.

## 9. Inclusive Writing and Medical Accuracy

YMYL content has additional requirements.

### Medical Accuracy

- Never give specific treatment advice without attribution to the doctor.
- Use "talk to your dentist" or "Dr. [Name] will assess during consultation" for individualized claims.
- Cite sources for general medical facts (ADA, CDC, AAP, peer-reviewed studies).
- Include "Last Medically Reviewed" date and reviewer doctor name on blog posts.

### Legal Safety

- Never promise specific outcomes ("you will love your new smile").
- Avoid "guaranteed" language on any medical or cosmetic procedure.
- For before/after images: include "Individual results may vary" per advertising guidelines.
- For cost ranges: always use ranges (not exact prices) unless the clinic has a fixed menu.

### Inclusivity

- Do not assume gender, age, or family structure in examples.
- Use "patients" rather than "ladies and gentlemen" or "moms and dads".
- Include examples that reflect the actual demographics of the clinic's community.

## 10. Final Human Sense Validation

Before delivery, read the entire piece out loud. Yes, out loud. This catches:

- Robotic rhythms.
- Missing human voice.
- Unnatural phrasing.
- Places where a real dentist would speak differently.

### The Questions to Ask

1. **Does this sound like a senior dental copywriter wrote it?** If no, rewrite.
2. **Could Dr. [Name] read this aloud to a patient in a consultation?** If no, rewrite.
3. **Are there any em dashes?** If yes, fix.
4. **Are there any AI transition phrases?** If yes, cut.
5. **Does every major section contain a practitioner insight?** If no, add.
6. **Is there any sentence that feels too polished or too generic?** If yes, rewrite with specificity.
7. **Does the page feel like it was written about this specific practice?** If no, add entity signals.

### The Sentence-Length Check

Count the words in the first 10 sentences. If more than 6 are within 2 words of each other in length, the rhythm is too uniform. Rewrite to vary.

### The AI Detector Check (Optional)

Run the draft through Originality.ai or GPTZero. Aim for 90%+ human. If under 85%, rewrite the flagged sections with more specificity, more practitioner voice, and more sentence variation.

## 11. Common AI Fingerprints to Hunt and Eliminate

Even after applying all rules above, scan for these residual patterns:

- **"Not only... but also..."** construction. Rewrite as two sentences.
- **"Both... and..."** construction overused. Vary with simple lists.
- **Every paragraph starting with the same transition word** ("The", "Our", "At").
- **Lists of three adjectives** ("safe, effective, and reliable"). Real writers vary.
- **Inspirational conclusions** ("Your smile awaits!"). Cut every one.
- **Overuse of colons** to introduce every explanation. Vary.
- **"Let's explore" / "Let's dive in"**. Delete.
- **Rhetorical questions** ("Did you know that...?"). Use sparingly or cut.
- **Repeating the question** from the H2 in the first sentence of the answer. Vary phrasing.

## 12. Human Writing Quick Check (Pre-Delivery)

Before marking any page complete, confirm all 10 items:

- [ ] Zero em dashes (search character `—`).
- [ ] Zero banned AI transition phrases.
- [ ] Sentence length varies across short, medium, and occasional long.
- [ ] No three sentences in a row with the same structure.
- [ ] Professional hedging used instead of absolutes (unless medically accurate).
- [ ] At least one practitioner insight sentence per major section.
- [ ] No generic marketing language ("passionate", "caring", "state-of-the-art", "world-class").
- [ ] Voice matches senior dental copywriter, not LLM.
- [ ] Every paragraph references entity, number, or local specific (if any generic paragraphs, rewrite).
- [ ] Read out loud: sounds natural, like a real person wrote it.

Content that fails any of these items is not A+. Rewrite before delivery.
